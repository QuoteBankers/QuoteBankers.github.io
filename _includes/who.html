Who you need to be

Isn't it a pleasure to be listened to? The ability to make your voice heard is a privilege that few can afford. Sometimes you can have the feeling that only the loudest are listened to. Starting from the 2015-2020 Quotebank dataset and the Wikidata dataset of speaker attributes, let us see the distribution of the key attributes of the quoted speakers.

1. Imbalance in number of quotations

As seen from the graph (log-scaled in the y-axis), most quotes are made by a few people, and most people have made a few quotes. In this dataset, 20% of the most quoted speakers have made 88% of the quotes! The distribution of the number of quotes per speaker is a power law, and interestingly agrees with the 80/20 rule (aka Pareto principle).
This law was observed by the Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto, whom while at the University of Lausanne (!), showed that 80% of the land in Italy was owned by 20% of the population. The correspondant is true for the case of the number of quotations in this dataset.

In analogy with wealth distribution, this would mean that most of the population is poor

In analogy with wealth distribution, this would mean that few people have ownership of most of the wealth

The Pareto Principle is often seen as a guide for how to allocate resources efficiently. Here, it gives us a window into which pursue our analysis.
After all, to answer the question who has a voice in the media?, we might as well focus on people dominating the dataset. Right?

2. Male-dominated quotations

In the pre-processed part of the dataset used to run the rest of our analysis, males stand for roughly 77% of all quotations, females for roughly 22% and other genders for a little over 1%. Even though the data presented has been aggregated over the years 2015-2020, we have not observed significant changes in the distribution of genders over these years.
